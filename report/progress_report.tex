\section*{Introduction}

Modern Vision-Language Models (VLMs) are massive, requiring billions of parameters and huge
GPU resources that make them impossible to run on everyday hardware or mobile devices. Mini-VLM
is a compact Vision-Language Model for Visual Question Answering (VQA) that we constrain to a
strict \textbf{100MB storage budget}, targeting edge deployment: given a $224\times224$ image
and a free-form text question as input, the model outputs one of the 1000 most common VQA
answers (Figure~\ref{fig:pipeline}). Running AI models on resource-constrained hardware requires
aggressive compression, and mapping raw pixels and free-form text jointly onto a correct answer
requires learned, non-linear visual and linguistic representations that hand-coded rules cannot
approximate (Goyal et al., 2017), which is why a deep-learning approach is appropriate here.
Rather than shrinking a Transformer-based VLM post hoc, our core contribution is a \emph{unified
RWKV pipeline}: an RWKV-based spatial bridge and an RWKV language core replace attention
everywhere in the model, giving linear-time (not quadratic) inference cost in the combined
visual+text sequence length (Peng et al., 2023), instead of the linear-projector-into-attention
approach used by prior connectors such as LLaVA (Liu et al., 2023).

Figure~\ref{fig:pipeline} shows the implemented pipeline: the frozen vision encoder is run
once, offline, and cached, and only the bridge and language core are trained.

\section*{Data Processing}

We use the official \textbf{VQA v2.0} dataset (questions/annotations over COCO images), cleaned
in two steps to fit a 100MB budget: (1) collapse free-form answers to a \textbf{top-1000
most-common-answer classification} problem, built from the frequency distribution over the full
443{,}757-example train2014 pool (87.47\% coverage), dropping any example outside that
vocabulary; (2) resize/normalize images to $224\times224$ and pass them through the frozen
vision encoder \emph{once, offline}, caching the resulting $576\times7\times7$ feature map
(FP16) so training never re-runs the CNN. We develop on a small, reproducible CPU subset, then
train the same architecture at production scale on GPU; Table~\ref{tab:stats} reports both.

\textbf{Train/val/test split.} \texttt{train}/\texttt{val} are disjoint samples from the
official \emph{train2014} pool; \texttt{test} is sampled entirely from \emph{val2014} -- images
and questions never seen during training or hyperparameter selection, our stand-in for
``never-before-seen'' data at this stage. For final testing we plan to submit predictions to the
official blind VQA v2.0 \emph{test2015} evaluation server, whose answers are not public.

cleaning: image, question, and the multiple-choice answer used as the classification target.

\textbf{Challenges.} COCO images for the CPU subset are fetched individually to avoid 13--19GB
zip archives; the full-scale run instead downloads and extracts those zips once, more reliable
at $\sim$204{,}000 images. Separately, a batch of cached feature tensors was silently corrupted
during extraction, spiking the training loss until we added a feature-integrity check
(\texttt{mini\_vlm/data/check\_features.py}) that now runs before training.

\section*{Baseline Model}

The baseline is the ``naive integration'' control group from the proposal: the same frozen
vision encoder, but a plain \textbf{Linear Projector} maps all 49 spatial patch tokens (no
compression) into a standard, unshared \texttt{nn.TransformerEncoder} (4 layers, 4 heads,
$d_{model}=128$, pre-LN) alongside the question's word embeddings; the final token's hidden
state is classified into 1000 answers with cross-entropy loss, no manual tuning beyond standard
defaults (AdamW, $lr=3\times10^{-3}$, linear warmup).

\textbf{Quantitative results.} On the full-scale GPU run (10 epochs, $\sim$10 min), held-out
accuracy reaches $\approx$\textbf{46\%}, well above the $\approx$20\% majority-class floor,
validation loss flattening around epoch 5 while training loss keeps falling. On our CPU dev
subset (1{,}247{,}080 params, 4.78MB, 30 epochs, $\sim$5 min) the pattern reproduces: train loss
2.60, test accuracy \textbf{28.5\%} vs. the 20.33\% floor (Figure~\ref{fig:comparison_curves},
Table~\ref{tab:comparison}). The full-scale model grows to $\approx$10MB, driven by the larger
question-tokenizer vocabulary, not the architecture itself.

\textbf{Qualitative results.} Table~\ref{tab:qualitative} (below, ``baseline'' column) shows
representative held-out predictions. Early on, an unshared Transformer trained from scratch was
fragile -- post-LN produced loss spikes/NaNs, fixed via pre-LN with linear warmup -- and the
baseline collapsed to one high-frequency answer regardless of question. Now it gives varied,
if often low-confidence, answers, occasionally the more conservative (and correct) model.

\section*{Primary Model}

\textbf{Vision Encoder.} Frozen, pretrained MobileNetV3-Small, compressed FP32$\to$FP16 (3.66MB
$\to$ 1.87MB). INT8 post-training static quantization hit an operator-support gap instead
(MobileNetV3's Squeeze-and-Excite blocks need a quantized elementwise multiply this
environment's quantized-CPU kernels don't implement end to end); FP16 is a reliable substitute,
INT8/GPTQ (Frantar et al., 2023) a concrete next step.

\textbf{RWKV Spatial Bridge (our main contribution).} RWKV time-mixing (WKV linear-time
recurrence, custom CUDA kernel for GPU) and channel-mixing (squared-ReLU FFN), from scratch --
no attention. It flattens the $576\times7\times7$ feature map into 49 tokens, projects to
$d_{model}=128$, runs 2 RWKV blocks, then compresses 49 tokens to 8 language-aligned tokens via
a \emph{learned linear pooling matrix} ($8\times49$ directly-learned weights, not a dynamic
cross-attention matrix).

\textbf{RWKV Language Core.} The 8 visual tokens concatenate with question word embeddings and
pass through a 4-layer RWKV stack; the final hidden state feeds a linear classifier over 1000
answers. Total (CPU dev subset): \textbf{1{,}742{,}064 parameters}, 6.69MB (comparable to the
baseline's 1{,}247{,}080); at full scale the larger tokenizer vocabulary grows this to
$\approx$11MB, still well inside budget.

\textbf{Quantitative results.} On the full-scale GPU run, held-out accuracy reaches
$\approx$\textbf{47\%} ($\sim$10 minutes total, about the same wall-clock time as the
baseline), essentially matching the baseline's $\approx$46\% rather than clearly exceeding it;
unlike the primary model, the baseline's full-scale validation loss is still slowly falling at
epoch 10, i.e. only the primary model is clearly overfitting yet at that scale. On our CPU dev
subset, validation accuracy favors the primary model for most of training (final epoch: 30.5\%
vs. 28.7\%) and its training loss falls much lower (0.51 vs. 2.60), but the ranking flips on the
600-example held-out test split (26.8\% vs. 28.5\%, Table~\ref{tab:comparison}) -- a reminder
that small test splits are noisy, and part of why the full-scale run's $\approx$187K-question
test split is the more trustworthy number.

\textbf{Qualitative results.} Table~\ref{tab:qualitative} (``primary'' column) shows the same
questions: when right, the primary model is very confident (\emph{snow}, 0.99), sharper than
the baseline's near-uniform confidence, but that confidence makes mistakes more severe (``2'' at
0.88 confidence for a ground-truth count of 1) -- consistent with a frozen encoder and only 8
compressed visual tokens having no way to represent fine-grained counts.

\textbf{Challenges \& feasibility.} The WKV recurrence's stable form (max-shifted sums, Peng et
al. 2023) needed care to avoid overflow. Surprisingly, the primary model overfits \emph{more}
than the baseline despite similar parameter counts; we added dropout inside every RWKV
sub-block (previously unregularized, unlike \texttt{nn.TransformerEncoder}'s default), not yet
validated at full scale. Both models beat the majority-class floor by $\approx$26 points within budget and
$\sim$10 min/10 epochs -- further scaling is feasible.
